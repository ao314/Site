Leaf Veneration Structures under Stochastic Damage

Winter 2026 | UCLA

Vein networks in leaves use hydraulic pressure to pull water into the entire leaf; under stress–such as a drought–this pressure may cause air embolisms to form across the leaf which damage and block specific pathways in the leaf. This structure forms an interesting model because the more water flowing through a vein the more likely it is to experience embolisms. Previous papers have failed to adequately consider this condition, instead focusing on uniformly random bond destruction. Additionally, stochastic techniques in gradient based optimization developed through research in artificial intelligence applications provides an interesting avenue to further optimize the process for computing these optimal networks. I used machine learning techniques such as back-propogation and auto-differentiation to model these veins within leaves as networks with stochastic damage applied in order to model the random formation of air embolisms.

Predicting Enrollment at The University of Iowa Using Adversarially Debiased Neural Networks and Ensemble Trees

Summer 2025 | University of Iowa

Extends ongoing research at the University of Iowa focused on predicting incoming undergraduate student enrollment throughout the admissions cycle. This process is complicated due to important predictors of enrollment that change over time, and the outcome of interest is observed much less frequently than the predictor information. Addressing these challenges, this work explores the concept of adversarial debiasing in neural network models to extract generalizable predictions from input data from multiple years; the results are then evaluated against currently used models, such as random forests and gradient boosted trees
